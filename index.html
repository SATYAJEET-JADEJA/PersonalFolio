About

I am Satyajeetsinh Jadeja, holding a bachelor's degree in electrical engineering from Sardar Vallabhbhai National Institute of Technology in India. Subsequently, I seized an excellent career opportunity as a Research and Development Testing Engineer at the European company HILTI. During my year-long tenure, my enthusiasm grew towards power electronics and the miniaturization of power electronic devices, leading me to delve into the world of semiconductors. Consequently, I resolved to pursue a Master's degree at the University at Buffalo, specializing in electrical and electronics engineering with a focus on solid-state electronics.

My Master's program provided me with an in-depth understanding of semiconductor chips, unravelling the underlying physics governing their operation, diverse semiconductor chip fabrication techniques, and their contemporary applications in the realm of electronics.

My roles encompassed working in the domains of power electronics, semiconductor device analysis, sensor fabrication, and their practical applications. Moreover, I gained valuable managerial experience in my capacity as a Systems Engineer.

EE 598 Individual problem under professor Dr. Huamin Li

In the Spring of 2022, I collaborated with Dr. Huamin Li within his research group, focusing on "Field-effect Raman Spectroscopic Investigation on Two-Dimensional MoS2." Engaging in various projects alongside fellow research group members proved to be an exceptional highlight of my Master's program, serving as a profound source of motivation to intensify my pursuit of knowledge in semiconductor physics. Throughout the entire duration of the Individual Problem Course, my responsibilities encompassed conducting experiments, preparing group presentations, and delivering these presentations during weekly meetings with fellow members of the research group. Under the guidance of Dr. Huamin Li, a nurturing educational environment was cultivated, fostering mutual learning through the exchange of research insights and experiences.

In the new world of emerging technology semiconductor plays an important role. Two-dimensional (2D) materials are the new to go around of Moore’s law, and MoS2 has been considered one of the most promising channel materials due to its superior semiconductor properties compared to conventional Si at nanometre scales. In this work, we exploit an electric-field Raman scanning spectroscopy to investigate electro-optical effects on MoS2. By applying either in-plane or out-of-plane electric fields, the vibration modes can be revealed and manipulated. Such electrical-optical integrated characterization will generate a new degree of freedom to understand the phonon dynamics in 2D materials, paving a way toward the realization of novel nanoelectronics based on 2D materials.



As illustrated in the accompanying figure, I undertook the task of fabricating multiple transistors using MoS2 material. My objective was to conduct a comprehensive array of tests and gather extensive data to elucidate how the material's properties evolve and how we can harness these transformations to our advantage.



The Individual Problem Course led by Dr. Huamin Li emerged as a standout experience during my pursuit of a Master's degree. I wholeheartedly endorse the participation of every student in such individual problem courses, as they afford the opportunity to embark on entirely novel research projects or contribute to ongoing research efforts of Ph.D. students. This course not only shed light on the enjoyable, challenging, and rewarding nature of research work but also bestowed upon me a profound sense of gratitude for the invaluable educational opportunities it provided.

EE 541LEC Special Topics Course Topic: Solid State Sensors

My inherent fascination has always been with semiconductor devices and their multifarious applications, with sensors representing one of the paramount and pivotal applications of semiconductor devices. The course in Solid-State Sensors provided an extensive exploration of electronic sensors, encompassing their operational principles, diverse applications, and the intricacies of sensor fabrication within a cleanroom environment. Electronic sensors play an integral role in an array of applications, spanning from rudimentary temperature sensing to the orchestration of highly automated systems. In this course I learned about the semiconductor fabrications, characterization and its applications across various categories of solid-state sensors, including acoustic, mechanical, electrical, magnetic, radiation, thermal, chemical, and biological sensors. The core focus of this course was to learn about sensor fabrication processes and the fundamental principles underpinning their operation

Furthermore, during this course, I was given an opportunity to take up a topic of my choice and do literature review of that topic. I conducted a comprehensive review of recent scholarly literature pertaining to Piezoelectric Pressure sensors. These endeavours facilitated my understanding of the operational principles of these pressure sensors, delving even into their functioning at the nano scale. As illustrated in the accompanying figure, a piezoelectric semiconductor has the capability to generate remnant polarization at its surface or interface with other materials when subjected to mechanical strain due to the direct piezoelectric effect. This phenomenon, coupled with the insufficient screening of polarization by free charges, induces a redistribution of free charge carriers and modulates the band structures in proximity to the interface. This, in turn, enables the manipulation of electronic transport across the interface through dynamic mechanical inputs. This phenomenon is formally known as the "piezotronics effect," delineating the profound connection between piezoelectricity and electrical transport within piezoelectric semiconductors.


In addition, I had the privilege of gaining insights into the intricate fabrication process of Piezoelectric Pressure sensors, as elucidated in the following figure. This process entails a sequence of steps, each involving specialized machinery and techniques, such as wafer preparation, wet etching, doping, atomic layer deposition (ALD), electron beam lithography (EBL), metal deposition, wet/dry etching, sputtering, photolithography, spin coating, plasma-enhanced chemical vapor deposition (PECVD), dry transfer, wet transfer, and more.



My knowledge was further enriched by a comprehensive exploration of the diverse applications of these sensors, their deployment scenarios, market trends, and the anticipated growth trajectory in this domain.



Upon completion of this course, I not only acquired a profound comprehension of the working principles and applications of solid-state sensors but also developed a profound interest in the semiconductor manufacturing industry. Semiconductors constitute the fundamental building blocks of virtually every electronic device, amplifying my fascination with this industry. I wholeheartedly recommend this course to fellow electrical engineering students, and I would eagerly undertake it again. The realm of sensors offers an abundance of opportunities for learning and practical application, making it an immensely rewarding educational experience. For students aspiring to specialize in the field of solid-state electronics, this course is not only beneficial but also adds significant value to their curriculum vitae.
